1. Introduction to Sound Synthesis

Welcome to SynthDeck! Sound synthesis is the art and science of creating audio signals from scratch using electronic circuits or digital algorithms.

In sound production, we categorize synthesis into four main methods, each producing unique timbral characteristics:

  • Subtractive Synthesis: Starts with geometrically rich waveforms containing many harmonics, then uses filters to cut away unwanted frequencies (subtracted energy).
  • Frequency Modulation (FM): Modulates the frequency of one sound wave (carrier) using another high-frequency wave (modulator), generating complex metallic or bell-like textures.
  • Physical Modeling: Uses mathematical simulations of acoustic physical properties (like strings, pipes, or wooden blocks) to replicate real-world instruments.
  • Wavetable Synthesis: Scans through a pre-recorded table of digital cycles, morphing smoothly between different waveshapes over time.

Use the selector on the right to hear how these methods differ!

2. Anatomy of a Synthesizer

Most commercial subtractive synthesizers share a standardized signal routing path. To understand sound design, we must follow this structural flow:

  • VCO (Oscillator): The primary generator. It produces the raw periodic electrical sound waves (sawtooth, square, triangle) at a specific pitch.
  • VCF (Filter): The tone-sculptor. It shapes the overall tone by cutting out or emphasizing frequency bands (e.g. low-pass filtering).
  • VCA (Amplifier): The volume gate. It controls how the loudness changes over time (e.g. key press to fade out).
  • Modulators (LFO / Envelopes): Auxiliary control sources that modulate parameters of the VCO, VCF, or VCA, adding movement and expression.

Hover or click on the modules in the block diagram to isolate their sonic functions in real-time!

3. The Oscillator (VCO)

The Voltage Controlled Oscillator (VCO) is the starting point of sound. It produces a repeating electrical wave shape. The speed of this repetition is called frequency, which we measure in Hertz (Hz) or cycles per second.

Our ears perceive frequency as pitch:

  • Higher Frequency: Faster repeating cycles, resulting in a higher musical pitch.
  • Lower Frequency: Slower repeating cycles, resulting in a deeper musical pitch.

Press the keys on the keybed or use your keyboard (keys A through K) to trigger a pure sine wave. Use Z to shift one octave down and X to shift one octave up. Observe the visual cycles on the oscilloscope canvas!

4. Additive Synthesis

A pure sine wave is the only sound wave that contains a single, isolated frequency. All other complex waveforms are composed of a combination of sine waves added together.

This is the foundation of **Additive Synthesis**:

  • Fundamental Pitch: The lowest frequency present in a sound, which determines the overall musical note we hear.
  • Harmonics: Higher frequencies that are whole-number multiples of the fundamental pitch (e.g., 2x, 3x, 4x). These shape the unique tone color (timbre).

Adjust the harmonic sliders on the right. Mix in the harmonics to build a complex tone. Press keys A-K to play, and use Z/X to shift octaves. Try the Flute, Clarinet, and Organ presets to see their spectrums!

5. Waveforms & The Pulse Wave

Subtractive synthesis relies on geometric waveforms that contain rich sets of harmonics. The primary shapes are:

  • Sawtooth: Bright and buzzy, containing all integer harmonics (both odd and even). Ideal for strings and brass.
  • Triangle: Warm and flute-like, containing only odd harmonics that roll off very rapidly.
  • Pulse (or Square) Wave: Hollow and woody. Its harmonic makeup depends on its Pulse Width—the percentage of time the wave stays at its peak compared to its trough.

Adjust the Pulse Width slider on the right between 10% and 50%. Play notes using A-K and shift octaves with Z/X. Watch the duty cycle narrow on the oscilloscope, hearing it shift from a hollow square wave to a nasal pulse buzz.

6. Unpitched Noise Generation

Not all sounds are periodic. Periodic sounds have repeating waveforms that create a clear musical pitch. Non-periodic sounds are random, resulting in noise.

In sound production, noise is essential for synthesizing drums, wind, rain, and sound effects. We classify noise by its frequency content:

  • White Noise: Contains equal energy across all individual frequencies. It sounds bright, harsh, and hiss-like (analogous to white light containing all colors).
  • Pink Noise: Contains equal energy per octave. Because our hearing logarithmic, pink noise sounds balanced, natural, and deep—similar to ocean waves or rushing wind.

Use the crossfader slider to morph between White and Pink noise and study the FFT spectrum curves!

7. Introduction to Modulation

A static synthesizer sound is boring and flat. To breathe life and movement into a sound, we use modulation—automatically changing a parameter over time.

Every modulation setup requires three main concepts:

  • Modulation Source: The generator of the control signal (e.g. an LFO or an Envelope).
  • Modulation Destination: The target parameter being controlled (e.g. Oscillator Pitch, Filter Cutoff, or VCA Volume).
  • Modulation Depth: The strength or range of the modulation.

Drag a patch cable on the right from a Source port (LFO/Envelope) to a Destination input port (Pitch/Filter/Volume) to see how the modulation sweeps the target dials!

8. Envelope Generators (ADSR)

An Envelope Generator is a modulator that produces a single control curve triggered every time a key is pressed. The standard envelope is divided into four stages (ADSR):

  • Attack (A): The time it takes for the signal to rise from zero to its maximum level.
  • Decay (D): The time it takes to fall from the peak level to the sustain level.
  • Sustain (S): The steady volume level maintained as long as the key is held down.
  • Release (R): The time it takes for the sound to fade to zero after the key is released.

Visualizing Envelope Stages: Drag the visual envelope nodes (Attack, Decay, Sustain, and Release) on the right to shape your sound envelope. Play notes using keys A-K and shift octaves with Z/X. Watch the orange tracker ball follow the envelope path in real-time: it rises through the Attack stage, descends through Decay, sits at Sustain as long as you hold the key, and slides down the Release path once you release the key!

9. Filter Types & Slopes

A Voltage Controlled Filter (VCF) is used to subtract frequency bands from a sound, sculpting its timbre. The point where the filter starts cutting frequencies is called the Cutoff Frequency.

Filters behave differently depending on their type:

  • Low-Pass (LPF): Cuts high frequencies and lets low frequencies pass. Perfect for making warm, mellow, or bass-heavy tones.
  • High-Pass (HPF): Cuts low frequencies and lets high frequencies pass. Ideal for bright, thin, or airy textures.
  • Band-Pass (BPF): Cuts both highs and lows, passing only a narrow frequency band.

The steepness of the low-pass filter's cutoff is measured in poles (stages). Each pole adds -6 dB/octave of slope:

  • 1-Pole: Gentle roll-off (-6 dB/octave).
  • 2-Pole: Moderate roll-off (-12 dB/octave).
  • 4-Pole: Steep, classic analog roll-off (-24 dB/octave).

Play notes (keys A-K) and sweep the Cutoff slider on the right to audition the filter types and low-pass slopes without any resonance!

10. Filter Resonance (Q)

Resonance (also known as Q, or quality factor) is created by feeding a portion of the filter's output back into its input.

This feedback loop creates a sharp volume boost (or peak) right at the Cutoff Frequency. As you sweep the cutoff frequency, this resonant peak emphasizes the harmonics crossing it, producing a characteristic nasal "honk", whistle, or wah-wah sweep.

If resonance is turned up high enough, the filter will self-oscillate, generating its own clean tone even with no incoming oscillator sound!

Use the 2D XY Pad on the right to sweep the filter in real-time. The X-axis controls the Cutoff frequency, while the Y-axis controls the Resonance level.

11. Low Frequency Oscillators (LFO)

An LFO is an oscillator that operates below the threshold of human hearing (usually under 20 Hz). Because we can't hear it directly, we use it to modulate other components:

  • Pitch Modulation (Vibrato): Creates a subtle, warm pitch warble.
  • Volume Modulation (Tremolo): Pulsates the volume output.
  • Filter Modulation (Wah/Growl): Creates sweeping filter sweeps, central to dubstep basslines and ambient pads.

Adjust the LFO Rate, Depth, and Destination parameters on the right. Play notes using keys A-K (shift octaves with Z/X) to hear the LFO modulate pitch (vibrato), filter cutoff (wah-wah), or volume (tremolo) in real-time!
